That's a much more interesting angle — and frankly a more defensible cognitive science thesis. The first framing was "reduce cognitive load," which is fine but well-trodden. This one is asking a harder question: can the interface be designed to strengthen cognition rather than just making things easier?

The tension you're identifying is real and under-explored. Most AI-assisted UI design is heading toward offloading — autocomplete your thoughts, hide complexity, minimize effort. But there's a well-established body of cognitive science that says effort is where learning and cognitive development happen. Desirable difficulties (Bjork), generation effects, active recall, productive struggle — all of these say that making things too easy can atrophy the very faculties you'd want to preserve.

So the research question becomes something like: How can an intent-driven, dynamically adaptive interface be designed to scaffold and strengthen human cognitive faculties — such as reasoning, planning, and mental model construction — rather than offloading them?

The idea is that the system interprets your intent, but instead of just giving you the answer or surfacing the exact right view, it strategically introduces friction. Maybe it asks you to articulate your goal more precisely before surfacing options. Maybe it shows you a partial view and asks you to predict what's missing. Maybe it tracks your interaction patterns and gradually removes scaffolding as you demonstrate mastery — like cognitive training wheels that retract.

This maps onto rich cognitive science territory: Vygotsky's zone of proximal development, scaffolding theory (Wood, Bruner, Ross), cognitive apprenticeship (Collins, Brown, Newman), self-regulated learning, and metacognition. You're essentially proposing an interface that acts as a cognitive tutor rather than a cognitive prosthetic.

For the proof-of-concept, you could build a small system that demonstrates both modes — an "offloading" mode that just gives you what you need, and a "strengthening" mode that scaffolds you toward finding it yourself — and compare them on something measurable like transfer performance or self-efficacy.

This is a much sharper thesis. Want me to draft the pitch around this framing?

TURN 6 - CLAUDE:
Oh, that's a strong addition — and it actually sharpens the cognitive science thesis rather than just adding visual flair. Let me think through what scroll buys you here.

The key reframe: right now your paradigm is conversation-as-primary-surface, with widgets materializing in place. Adding a scrollable canvas gives you a spatial dimension on top of the temporal one. That matters cognitively because spatial memory is one of the strongest, most durable forms of human memory — the method of loci works precisely because we're built to remember where things are far better than what order they came in. So a canvas where intent-surfaced widgets get placed in space rather than just streamed in time lets users build a spatial mental model of their own problem-solving session. That's a faculty-strengthening move, not an offloading one.

And scroll specifically is interesting because scroll is self-paced traversal. Unlike an animation that plays at you, scroll-driven progression puts the user in control of the rate — which connects directly to your desirable-difficulties framing. The user has to actively move through the material, and the act of scrolling back to revisit something is itself a retrieval event.

Here are the directions I see, roughly ordered by how well they serve the strengthening thesis:

Scroll as scaffolding fade. This is the best fit. As the user scrolls forward through a task, the scaffolding progressively thins — early in the scroll, the canvas shows full prompts, hints, worked structure; further down, those elements fade out and the user is working more unaided. Scroll position literally is the fading mechanism from scaffolding theory. You're spatializing the ZPD: the top of the canvas is "supported," the bottom is "independent," and the user's progress down the page is their progress toward autonomy.

Spatial persistence as external memory you build, not borrow. Instead of widgets vanishing when the conversation moves on, they get parked on the canvas at a location. The user scrolls back to find their earlier reasoning. The distinction from offloading: the system isn't remembering for them, it's giving them a space to construct and navigate their own externalized model — closer to a notebook than an autocomplete. Hutchins' distributed cognition is the frame, but you keep the human as the one doing the structuring.

Scroll-triggered prediction prompts. As the user scrolls toward a region where a result/widget will appear, the system first asks them to predict what comes next before revealing it on continued scroll. The generation effect baked into the navigation gesture itself. This is friction precisely located at the moment of revelation.

Progressive disclosure tied to demonstrated understanding. The canvas doesn't let you scroll past a section until you've engaged with it (answered, articulated, predicted). Scroll becomes a gate, not just a scrubber — which enforces the productive struggle instead of letting users skip it.

One caution worth holding: scroll-driven effects are seductive and can easily tip into spectacle — parallax-y, look-at-me motion that adds extraneous cognitive load rather than reducing it, which would directly contradict your thesis. The discipline is that every scroll behavior has to earn its place by either (a) spatializing a mental model, (b) implementing scaffolding fade, or (c) inserting a desirable difficulty. If a scroll effect doesn't do one of those three, it's decoration and it's working against you.

For the experiment, the scrollable-canvas version actually gives you a cleaner manipulation: your offloading mode is a streamed, ephemeral chat (answer appears, scrolls away, gone), while your scaffolding mode is the spatial canvas where the user constructs and re-traverses their reasoning. The transfer-task prediction gets sharper — if spatial construction strengthens the mental model, the canvas group should transfer better.
